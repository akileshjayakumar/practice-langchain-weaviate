Walt Disney, born on December 5, 1901, in Chicago, Illinois, was an American entrepreneur, animator, and film producer. He co-founded The Walt Disney Company with his brother Roy O. Disney. Walt Disney revolutionized the entertainment industry by creating iconic characters like Mickey Mouse and pioneering the field of animated films. Notable achievements include the creation of the first synchronized sound cartoon, "Steamboat Willie" (1928), and the first full-length animated feature, "Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs" (1937).

Disney expanded his empire with the creation of Disneyland in 1955, a theme park in Anaheim, California, and later Disney World in Florida, which opened after his death. His innovative contributions to animation, film, and theme parks left a lasting legacy in entertainment and popular culture. Disney won 22 Academy Awards during his career, more than any other individual, and his influence endures through the global success of The Walt Disney Company.
